Ancient Chinese medicine, with roots dating back over 2,000 years, significantly impacted the world through the spread of acupuncture, herbal remedies, and holistic health concepts (Yin-Yang, Qi) along the Silk Road. It influenced medical practices in Asia (Korea, Japan) and eventually the West, introducing techniques like pulse diagnosis, herbal medicine, and cupping.

Global Spread via Trade: The Silk Road facilitated the exchange of medical knowledge, with Chinese herbal medicine (e.g., rhubarb, ginseng) and acupuncture techniques spreading to Central Asia, the Middle East, and eventually Europe and America.

Acupuncture and Techniques: Acupuncture, tracing back to the Stone Age, has become a globally recognized, evidence-based complementary therapy for pain management, evolving from stone needles to modern, specialized techniques. Influence on Asian Medicine: Before the 6th century, Chinese medicine deeply influenced Korean and Japanese medical systems, which adopted similar theoretical foundations. Herbal Medicine and Pharmacology: The Bencao Gangmu, a 16th-century Chinese pharmacopoeia, was known internationally, with thousands of herbal remedies contributing to traditional and modern pharmacological knowledge. Cultural Diplomacy: Historically, the dissemination of Chinese medicine served as a tool for cultural exchange, establishing its reputation as a sophisticated, long-standing medical system.

Modern Integration: Today, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) is practiced globally (in over 183 countries) and is integrated into some modern healthcare systems as a,ccomplementary approach for chronic illness and wellness.
